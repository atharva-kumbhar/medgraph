Welcome to the GraphRAG Inference Hackathon by TigerGraph!

A beginner-friendly online coding challenge where you'll prove that graphs 
make LLM inference faster, cheaper, and smarter than vector-based RAG alone.


About the Opportunity:

LLMs are spreading rapidly across every industry, and as they do, token 
consumption is exploding. Companies pay more, wait longer, and hit context 
limits faster every quarter. This isn't an academic exercise. It's a real, 
growing pain in production AI today.

The GraphRAG Inference Hackathon by TigerGraph is your chance to prove that 
graphs solve this. You'll build three pipelines side-by-side (raw LLM, Basic 
RAG, and GraphRAG) and let the numbers tell the story.

The headline metric: token reduction with maintained accuracy. Show us how 
much GraphRAG cuts down without dropping answer quality, and you'll have 
built the kind of project that gets hiring managers, AI teams, and the 
open-source community paying attention.

New to GraphRAG? No problem. This hackathon is designed to be 
beginner-friendly. You don't need prior experience with TigerGraph or graph 
databases. If you know Python and have played with LLMs or APIs, you're 
ready to build. We provide documentation, starter resources, and 1:1 
mentoring sessions for the Top 10 teams.

Whether you're a student exploring GenAI, a developer curious about graphs, 
or an AI engineer building production RAG systems, this hackathon is your 
playground to learn, build, and compete.


What You will Build:

Three pipelines that answer the same questions on the same data, plus an 
interactive comparison dashboard.

Pipeline 1: LLM-Only (Baseline). A prompt goes in, an output comes out. 
No retrieval. Your worst-case baseline.
Pipeline 2: Basic RAG (Vector + LLM). Vector embeddings retrieve similar 
chunks, the LLM generates an answer using that context. The current 
industry standard.
Pipeline 3: GraphRAG (Graph + LLM). Built on top of the TigerGraph 
GraphRAG repo. TigerGraph extracts entities, maps relationships, and 
performs multi-hop reasoning. The LLM generates the final answer using 
this filtered, structured context, not a giant document dump.
Comparison Dashboard: An interactive page where you enter one query and 
all three pipelines respond side-by-side. Tracks tokens used, response 
latency, cost per query, and answer accuracy (evaluated using LLM-as-a-Judge 
+ BERTScore via Hugging Face). One query in, three answers + metrics out, 
side-by-side. That's the story.

Sign up here and there accuracy checking page -# Accuracy Evaluation Guide

Building three pipelines and showing token reduction is only half the story. The other half is proving that **GraphRAG's answers are actually good** — not just shorter, but accurate and trustworthy.

Every team is required to evaluate answer quality using **two complementary Hugging Face approaches**. Together they tell us whether your GraphRAG pipeline is genuinely winning or just cutting corners.

---

## Why Two Methods?

A single accuracy metric can be gamed or misleading. Using two independent methods gives you robust, defensible accuracy numbers and shows judges you took evaluation seriously.

---

## Method 1: LLM-as-a-Judge (Primary)

You ask another AI to grade your answers.

For each question in your evaluation set, you send three things to a judge LLM (a free Hugging Face hosted model like Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct): the question, the correct answer, and your pipeline's answer. The judge returns a verdict: **PASS** or **FAIL**.

**What it captures:** Real-world correctness. Did the answer actually address the question? Did it hallucinate? Did it contradict the source? This is the closest you can get to a human grader.

**Tools:**

- `huggingface_hub.InferenceClient` to call hosted models
- Free monthly inference credits cover hackathon-scale evaluations (50–100 calls easily)
- Recommended judge: `meta-llama/Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct`

---

## Method 2: BERTScore (Validator)

You measure how semantically similar your answer is to the correct answer.

BERTScore uses an embedding model to compare the meaning of two pieces of text — even if they use completely different words. So *"Apple was founded by Steve Jobs and Steve Wozniak"* and *"Steve Jobs and Wozniak created Apple together"* score nearly identically.

**What it captures:** Semantic accuracy. Even when answers are reworded, BERTScore catches whether the meaning is preserved.

**Tools:**

- `evaluate.load("bertscore")` from the Hugging Face `evaluate` library
- Runs locally on CPU after a one-time model download
- Always set `rescale_with_baseline=True` for an honest 0–1 scale

---

## How to Set It Up

### Step 1: Build Your Ground-Truth Set (~20 minutes)

Hand-write or generate **30–50 questions** about your dataset with correct reference answers. Aim for variety: factual questions, multi-hop reasoning, and synthesis questions that require connecting multiple documents.

```json
[
  {
    "question": "Which two researchers collaborated on the 2023 paper about transformer efficiency?",
    "correct_answer": "Dr. Smith and Dr. Patel co-authored the paper."
  },
  {
    "question": "What was the impact of the merger between Company A and Company B?",
    "correct_answer": "The merger consolidated their market position and reduced operational costs by 40%."
  }
]
```

### Step 2: Run All 3 Pipelines (~30 minutes)

Send the same questions through your LLM-only, Basic RAG, and GraphRAG pipelines. Save every answer.

### Step 3: Evaluate Using Both Methods (~20 minutes)

```python
import os
import evaluate
from huggingface_hub import InferenceClient

# Setup
client = InferenceClient(
    model="meta-llama/Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct",
    token=os.environ["HF_TOKEN"]  # free Hugging Face account token
)
bertscore = evaluate.load("bertscore")

JUDGE_PROMPT = """Grade the system's answer.
Question: {q}
Correct answer: {correct}
System answer: {answer}

Reply with only PASS or FAIL.
PASS = the system answer correctly addresses the question with no major errors.
FAIL = the answer is wrong, missing, or contradicts the correct answer."""

def evaluate_pipeline(pipeline_outputs, ground_truth):
    judge_results = []
    
    for output, truth in zip(pipeline_outputs, ground_truth):
        # LLM-as-a-Judge
        prompt = JUDGE_PROMPT.format(
            q=truth["question"],
            correct=truth["correct_answer"],
            answer=output
        )
        verdict = client.chat_completion(
            [{"role": "user", "content": prompt}],
            max_tokens=10, temperature=0.0
        )
        judge_results.append("PASS" in verdict.choices[0].message.content.upper())
    
    # BERTScore (batch)
    bert_results = bertscore.compute(
        predictions=pipeline_outputs,
        references=[t["correct_answer"] for t in ground_truth],
        lang="en",
        rescale_with_baseline=True  # honest 0–1 scale
    )
    
    return {
        "llm_judge_pass_rate": sum(judge_results) / len(judge_results),
        "bertscore_f1": sum(bert_results["f1"]) / len(bert_results["f1"])
    }
```

### Step 4: Display in Your Dashboard

Show accuracy metrics for all three pipelines side-by-side, alongside tokens, latency, and cost.

---

## Bonus Points for High Accuracy

Strong accuracy isn't a hard requirement, but it's heavily rewarded. Submissions hitting these bars earn extra points in judging:

- High pass rate on the LLM-as-a-Judge evaluation (≥ 90%)
- Strong semantic similarity on BERTScore F1 rescaled (≥ 0.55) or its raw variant (≥ 0.88)
- Hitting both unlocks the maximum bonus

These thresholds represent a strong, defensible "good answers" bar:

- **90% pass rate** means 9 out of 10 answers are correct under independent AI grading
- **Rescaled BERTScore ≥ 0.55** indicates strong semantic similarity to the ground truth (rescaled scale: 0 = random, 1 = perfect)

> **Why this matters:** Token reduction without accuracy isn't a win — it's a regression. A team that cuts tokens by 70% but produces wrong answers hasn't proven anything. The bonus rewards teams that prove GraphRAG is genuinely better, not just cheaper.
